# MOVE 2 — Hypothesis

**The claim:**
Verbal or textual agreement with a diagnostic sentence is not, by itself, a valid signal of genuine recognition — regardless of how accurate the sentence is, how warmly the person responds, or whether they restate it in their own words. This holds even when the person's wording confirms the underlying mechanism (e.g. naming the specific fear or pattern themselves), because confirming a mechanism in words is still a verbal act, not a behavioral one. Genuine recognition is operationally defined as one of exactly two observable things: (a) an **unprompted behavior change** — the person does something afterward that they were not doing before (asks for help, ships the blocked feature, drops a comparison-shopping loop, books the thing they were avoiding), with no further nudging from the interviewer; or (b) an **unprompted escalation in the conversation itself** — a question like "how did you know that" or "okay, so what do I actually do about it," asked without being fished for, that signals the sentence produced new orientation rather than just confirmation. Everything else — "yeah," "kinda agree," "ok," "nicely said," "that's true," even fully self-aware agreement that names the mechanism back accurately — sits in a single undifferentiated bucket of **polite confirmation**, and this bucket cannot be used as evidence that a diagnostic sentence "worked," because it is also the default response to a sentence that tells someone something they already knew. The mechanism behind this claim: people are socially obligated to respond to a sincere, personal observation with some form of acknowledgment, and "agreement" is the lowest-cost acknowledgment available whether or not anything new actually landed — so the presence of agreement carries near-zero information about novelty, and only the presence of (a) or (b) does.

**Result that proves it right:**
In Move 5, the tool is run on 2 people, at least one of them cold/uncoached. The hypothesis is supported if **at least one of the two** produces a reaction falling into category (a) or (b) above — for example, they volunteer an action they're about to take that wasn't mentioned before the sentence was delivered, or they ask an unprompted "how did you know" / "what do I do now" question — and critically, that this reaction is visibly different in kind (not just in enthusiasm) from the flat-agreement pattern already logged in all three Move 1 interviews: Person 1 ("kinda agree... not wanting to take the risk" — restates the mechanism but takes no new action and asks nothing further), Person 2 ("ok" — minimal acknowledgment, explicitly described by him as something that already doesn't block him), and Person 3 ("nicely said" — warm, complimentary, but a closing statement, not an opening one). If even one Move 5 subject clears the bar into (a)/(b), that demonstrates the two categories (polite confirmation vs. genuine recognition) are real and distinguishable in practice, not just in theory — which is the entire premise the tool needs to be useful at all.

**Result that means no real effect exists (horoscope / mirror):**
Both Move 5 subjects respond with some form of polite confirmation only — "yeah that's right," "kinda true," "wow accurate," "interesting," possibly even enthusiastic or detailed agreement — but in both cases there is no unprompted action taken afterward and no unprompted follow-up question asked; any further detail in the conversation only comes because the interviewer keeps probing, not because the subject volunteers it. If this is what happens, it means that no matter how precisely targeted, novel, or well-delivered the diagnostic sentence is, the only observable output is the same low-information "polite agreement" bucket seen in Move 1 — i.e., there is no detectable behavioral signature that separates a sentence that truly landed from one that didn't. In that case the tool is functionally a horoscope: it can produce sentences that feel personally accurate, but it has no way to prove to anyone (including its own builder) that "feels accurate" and "is actually new information" are different things, because both produce the identical surface reaction.

**Kill-number:**
If, across both Move 5 subjects, there is zero instance of an unprompted action and zero instance of an unprompted "how did you know" / "what do I do now" — type follow-up (i.e., every reaction collected, even if positive, only happens because the interviewer asked for it or only consists of agreement language) — then write "my hypothesis was wrong." This is the specific, falsifiable failure condition: it would mean the central premise of this whole approach — that behavioral/escalation signal can be reliably separated from polite verbal signal — does not hold up even once in a live test, and the tool has no validation path beyond self-reported "yeah, accurate," which Move 1 already showed is uninformative and which the brief explicitly disqualifies as a real test of whether a diagnosis actually worked.
