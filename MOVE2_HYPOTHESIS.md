MOVE 2 — Hypothesis
The claim:

Verbal or textual agreement with a diagnostic sentence — even when the person says it's accurate, agrees, or restates it in their own words — is not a valid signal of genuine recognition. Real recognition only shows up as one of: (a) an unprompted behavior change (the person takes an action they weren't taking before), or (b) an unprompted follow-up like "how did you know that" or "so what do I do about it" — something that goes beyond confirming the sentence. Politeness, agreement, and even self-aware restatement ("yeah, that's exactly it") are the default response regardless of whether the sentence is actually novel to the person, and therefore cannot be used to validate a diagnostic tool.
Result that proves it right:

In Move 5, when the tool is run on 2 people (at least 1 cold/uncoached), at least one of them produces a reaction in category (a) or (b) above — an unprompted action or a "how did you know" / "what do I do" follow-up — and that reaction is distinguishable from the flat "yeah, agree" pattern seen in all 3 Move 1 interviews (Person 1: "kinda agree... not wanting to take the risk"; Person 2: "ok"; Person 3: "nicely said"). If even one Move 5 case clears that bar, it confirms that real recognition is a distinct, detectable category separate from polite agreement.
Result that means no real effect exists (horoscope / mirror):

Both Move 5 subjects respond with verbal agreement only — "yeah that's right," "kinda true," "interesting" — with no unprompted action and no unprompted follow-up question, regardless of how precisely or "newly" the sentence was targeted. If this happens, it means there's no reliably detectable difference between a diagnostic sentence that's genuinely landed and one that's just being politely accepted — i.e., the tool (and the underlying diagnosis approach) cannot be distinguished from a horoscope, because verbal feedback alone is uninformative no matter what's said.
Kill-number:

If both Move 5 subjects give only verbal agreement (no unprompted action, no unprompted "how did you know" / "what do I do" follow-up) — write "my hypothesis was wrong." This means the entire premise that behavioral signal can be separated from polite verbal signal fails, and the tool has no way to validate itself beyond user-reported "yeah, accurate," which the brief explicitly disqualifies as a real test.
